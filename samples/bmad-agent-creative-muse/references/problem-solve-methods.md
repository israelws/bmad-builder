# Problem-Solving Methods

Use these fluidly. Don't march through them sequentially — pick what fits the problem and the owner's energy. Switch methods when one isn't generating insight.

## Understanding the Problem (Before Solutions)

**Five Whys** — Ask "why" iteratively until you reach the root cause. Usually takes 3-7 layers. The first answer is almost never the real problem. Keep digging.

**Is/Is Not Analysis** — Define the problem by contrast. "It IS happening in production, it IS NOT happening in staging." "It IS affecting new users, it IS NOT affecting returning users." Boundaries reveal the real shape.

**Problem Statement Refinement** — Rewrite the problem statement 3 different ways. Each reframing suggests different solutions. "We're losing users" vs. "Users aren't finding value in the first 5 minutes" vs. "Our onboarding assumes knowledge users don't have."

**Systems Thinking** — Map the system the problem lives in. What feeds into it? What does it feed? Where are the feedback loops? Problems in systems are rarely where the symptoms appear.

**Fishbone (Ishikawa)** — Map causes by category: People, Process, Technology, Environment, Materials, Measurement. Prevents tunnel vision on the first cause that comes to mind.

## Analyzing Forces

**Force Field Analysis** — List driving forces (pushing toward solution) and restraining forces (pushing against). Don't just strengthen drivers — often weakening a restraint is easier and more effective.

**Constraint Mapping** — Identify every constraint: time, money, people, technology, politics, physics. Then question each: is it a real constraint or an assumed one? Assumed constraints kill more ideas than real ones.

**Pareto Analysis** — Which 20% of causes create 80% of the problem? Focus there first. Don't spread effort evenly across all causes.

## Generating Solutions

**TRIZ Inventive Principles** — 40 principles derived from analyzing millions of patents. When stuck, scan for applicable principles: segmentation, extraction, local quality, asymmetry, merging, universality, nesting, counterweight, prior action, etc. Each suggests a solution direction.

**Lateral Thinking** — Deliberately break patterns. Random entry point, provocation, challenge assumptions. The brain defaults to logical paths — force it off the path.

**Morphological Analysis** — Break the problem into independent dimensions. List options for each dimension. Combine across dimensions to generate novel solutions. Systematically covers the solution space.

**Biomimicry** — How has nature solved this? Resistance → streamlined shapes. Filtering → how kidneys work. Self-repair → how bones heal. Nature has had billions of years of R&D.

**Analogous Inspiration** — What domain has already solved this? "How do hospitals handle triage?" applied to customer support. Cross-domain solutions are almost always more creative than within-domain solutions.

## Evaluating Solutions

**Decision Matrix** — List options down, criteria across. Weight criteria by importance. Score each option. The math isn't the answer — the conversation about weights and scores reveals what actually matters.

**Pre-Mortem** — "It's one year from now and this solution failed completely. What went wrong?" Identify risks before they happen. People are better at explaining failure than predicting it.

**Cost-Benefit Quick Scan** — For each solution: what does it cost (time, money, effort, political capital)? What does it deliver? Is the ratio worth it compared to alternatives?

## Meta-Patterns

- **The presenting problem is rarely the real problem** — dig at least 2 layers deeper
- **Solutions that feel too easy probably are** — but solutions that feel impossible might just need reframing
- **If the owner keeps circling back to the same idea**, there's energy there worth exploring even if it seems impractical
- **When stuck, change the level of abstraction** — zoom out (what's the bigger system?) or zoom in (what's the specific moment of failure?)
- **Combine methods** — Five Whys to find root cause → Lateral Thinking to generate solutions → Pre-Mortem to stress-test. Methods are tools, not religions.
